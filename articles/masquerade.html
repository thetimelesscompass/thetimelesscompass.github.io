Secrets of The Masquarade
The Venetian Carnival is easily the most famous of all Italian carnivals, and is one of the most prominent carnivals of all time. It has been traced back to 1162 and had taken place continuously until it was banned in 1797. However, it was brought back in 1979 as part of the Italian government’s efforts to celebrate Venetian tradition and history. The carnival has been referred to a “season of excess, riot and social inversion”, due to the many extravagant, decadent and sinful activities that took place during the carnival. So what exactly were the types of activities that occurred at this carnival? ![](../static/img/masquerade/media/image5.png) *Carnevale di Venezia* #### Bull-baiting Bull-baiting, or *cacce dei tori*, was one of the more grandiose events of the carnival. In Piazza San Marco, the largest square in Venice located in front of the Doge’s (chief magistrate and most senior leader of the Venetian republic) palace, as many as a hundred bulls were released at once into the piazza. Afterwards, an “unmerciful” number of dogs were set loose to maul and attack the bulls. Supposedly, Corsican dogs, known to have been the fiercest breed in Italy, were bred specifically for the sport. ![](../static/img/masquerade/media/image6.png) *Cacce dei tori in Piazza San Marco* Bull-baiting was an expensive amusement for the Venetian aristocracy, especially the royalty, who were annual organisers of the event during the carnival. Interestingly, bull-baiting in Venice was treated as a festive event with a “high degree of emphasis on ritual, fancy dress, and athletic dexterity”. This was, however, a distinct contrast compared to what bull-baiting represented in other Italian states, where the peasants, who were symbolised by the dogs, were meant to rise up against the oppressive aristocrat, who were symbolised by the bull. #### Gambling The world’s first public gambling house, also known as *Il Ridotto*, originated from Venice in 1638. Built inside a wing of Palazzo Dandolo, it was highly frequented during the Venetian Carnival. Sanctioned by the government to control public gambling, it was open to all but discriminated against peasants and the middle-class by requiring players to wear three-cornered hats and masks, which were unaffordable to most. This meant that they had to find alternatives. Although gambling (with stakes) outside of the Ridotto was considered illegal, closed gambling clubs were popular with the less wealthy. ![](../static/img/masquerade/media/image4.png) *A gambling scene from Carlo Goldoni's play "Le Donne Gelose"* With Venice being one of the only (and likely the first) locations to offer gambling on a large and public scale, gambling became one of the biggest attractions for tourists. In particular, young men on their coming-of-age Grand Tour were known to make Venice a necessary pleasure stop. Despite being more famed for its elaborate masks and luxurious balls, the Carnevale di Venezia had many more activities and traditions that set it apart from any other festival in Europe. From gruesome and dark activities like bull-baiting to glamorous and excessive gambling, the carnival is said to be representative of an entertaining play, in which the people donned masks and became anonymous actors, partaking in indulgences and vices that were too fantastical to occur in ‘real-life’, when the curtains came down and their masks peeled off.
