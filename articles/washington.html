George Washington: Are His Monuments Untouchable?
Every Fourth of July, Americans of all race, creed, and religion come together to celebrate the independence of America, as well as the heroic struggles and sacrifices of the American Revolutionaries, especially the Commander-in-chief, George Washington. George Washington is perhaps one of the most revered historical figures in the world. There are memorials and statues commemorating the Founding Father all across the United States. Furthermore, there are artworks, poems, novels, films, buildings, and even universities created in his memory. Granted, almost every nation, especially those which became independent from an external ruler, celebrates their founding father. Founding Fathers are an integral aspect of the nation’s historical narrative as a sovereign state, not to mention it gives the people a personality to look up to. For instance, Haiti remembers its revolutionary hero Toussaint Louverture by printing his image on its currency; Cuba has erected multiple statues and busts of its National Hero José Martí – in fact, his birthplace, 41 Paula Street in Old Havana, is now a museum dedicated to Martí and his contribution towards Cuban independence. Indeed, more often than not, National Heroes tend to get immortalised and mythologised as their lives become the source of national history and pride. However, the extent to which America pays tribute to hers is certainly unparalleled. While it is a common practice to commemorate a national hero, it is definitely uncommon to elevate the hero to the likeness of Roman Emperors or Greek Gods. *George Washington* (1840) by Horatio Greenough exemplifies this. The tall marble sculpture was based on the legendary *Statue of Zeus at Olympia*, and depicts Washington in a godlike pose. Another example is *The Apotheosis of Washington* (1865) by Constantino Brumidi, a massive ceiling mural in the U.S Congress building. In this scene, Washington is seated at the centre of the heavens, donned in the royal colour purple, and surrounded by Roman gods and goddesses. ![](../static/img/washington/image.png) *Left: Image of the George Washington (1840) by Horatio Greenough, now owned by the Smithsonian Museum Right: Image of The Apotheosis of Washington (1865) by Constantino Brumidi, at the United States Congress building* The possible implications of these artworks are potentially unsettling: Washington had transcended humanity in these depictions, and is thus viewed as a godly hero to be revered by mortal Americans. One could even say that this is worryingly akin to how many dictators around the world glorify themselves in their cult of personality. However, monuments of George Washington have never come under the re-evaluation of historians nor the critical scrutiny of the public. For that matter, nobody really seems to mind such tributes made to Washington – but why? Before we can answer this question, it would be best to explore the purpose of monuments, and why they get preserved. Monuments are symbolic structures, often meant to commemorate a person or an event due to some sort of the significance that they pose. Critically, the longevity of a monument depends entirely on what it signifies. For instance, think of why citizens liberated from an authoritarian regime might tear down a statue of their former ruler. Despite the fact that the statute could be repurposed as a historical relic, they do so to get rid of the reminders of a system that they were victimised by and, consequently, despise. On the other hand, monuments that celebrate a legendary hero, for instance, tend to be preserved, because they are the reminder of a particular segment of historical past that the people wish to parade and celebrate. In other words, the citizenry may choose to preserve or take down monuments based on whether the monument represent the part of their history they wish to be represented or be rid of, respectively. Thus, the key to the question on whether George Washington’s monuments are problematic or not is to first understand what they represent. The artworks and structures still stand as legitimate monuments due to the following reasons: Firstly, monuments of George Washington commemorate not only the individual, but also the quintessential values of America. Almost every monument dedicated to a person applauds his or her achievements, and George Washington is no exception. Washington is remembered for his contributions to creating a system of democracy, freedom, and liberty in America, which are the very cornerstones on which the nation is built. Thus, we may look over the potentially controversial imagery – such as their propensity to overly venerate – that these monuments employ. Having said that, let us take another look at Greenough’s sculpture. The engravings on the sculpture are written in Latin, which translate to: “Horatio Greenough made this image as a great example of freedom, which will not survive without freedom itself”. Art Historians of Smithsonian Institute interpret the engraving as such: in conceptualising this sculpture, Greenough decided that Washington would be portrayed as a symbolic representation of liberty itself, which might explain the seemingly cryptic message that describes the statue of Washington as an “example of freedom”. Another important detail of the sculpture is how Washington is offering the ownership of the sword that he is holding to the Americans appreciating the statue, which symbolises his achievement of empowering the American people with democracy and autonomy. In other words, the statue was constructed to ultimately celebrate the American value of freedom, and the image of George Washington was a medium to convey this message. Therefore, George Washington as a representation of American values appears to outweigh any objections to the apotheosis of him as an individual. Thirdly, with regards to the seemingly problematic decision to portray Washington in the likeness of Zeus, Art Historians explain that it was meant to be a reference to Greek culture. As Greece was considered the origin of Western democracy, it was common for many Western artists during the early 19th century to adopt Greek art styles and imagery in works that celebrated democracy. Hence, in retrospect, we realise that our previous concerns with regards to Washington’s apotheosis were merely a misunderstanding based on our immediate reactions to the artworks, as we failed to understand the historic meaning and symbolic nature of these imagery and styles that were adopted in depicting Washington. Thirdly, Washington’s monuments are not as politically problematic as they were posthumously constructed in retrospect, rather than to reinforce an existing political regime. Sometimes, dictators build monuments of themselves as part of their cunning scheme of constructing a cult of personality so that they may eventually harness absolute political support from the masses. For instance, Turkmenistan’s dictator President Gurbangly Berdymukhamedov made news when he built a 69-foot golden statue to commemorate himself. Such monuments became a political tool to spread propaganda and aid the rule of despots, which would inevitably be associated with the despicable actions of the regimes in the future. In the case of Washington, however, most of his monuments were built centuries after his death, with full approval from both the Democrats and Republicans, and the general public. Therefore, monuments of Washington do not entail political bias or interests, and thus lack reason for them to be politically condemned. Lastly, unlike many megalomaniac or tyrannical leaders, George Washington’s legacy is deemed worthy of celebration and thus preservation by the American citizenry. This is perhaps attributed to the fact that Washington had to be convinced into accepting his Presidency, despite the fact that he had earned the unanimous vote of the electoral college and the unwavering support of the American public. Additionally, when he stepped down from Presidency in 1797, it was not due to any political pressure or scandal – he had genuinely volunteered to surrender his power. Hence, George Washington was a modest character who did not allow corruption or the desire for power to affect his work as a statesman. As a result, monuments dedicated to Washington are not the reminders of an oppressive leadership, but rather a reminder of a leader who was genuinely deserving of commemoration. This is not to negate the problematic aspects that monuments of George Washington entail. Indeed, celebrating a person means that we celebrate the whole person, meaning we inadvertently celebrate the terrible deeds the person has done as much as his or her commendable achievements. For instance, George Washington was a slave owner himself, coming from a family that built its wealth on tobacco plantations and slavery. Granted, he limited American involvement in the Atlantic slave trade, and was the only Founding Father to arrange the liberation of his slaves on his own volition, but he still partook in many of the abusive practices – such as whipping, caning, and beating – that were customary during this period. Moreover, the constructions of many of the monuments that were built in the earlier 19th century, such as the *Washington Monument* in Washington D.C, relied on slave labour. Yet, many of these unsettling, but unfortunately undeniable facts are overlooked when appreciating George Washington monuments. Personally, I am in no position of expertise or qualification to judge George Washington, or to conclude whether America should or need not be disturbed by Washington’s monuments. I cannot weigh his contributions in spearheading American independence in contrast to his personal life as a slave owner, as this results in a terrible dilemma: To argue that Washington’s contributions as a National Hero outweigh everything else would be to dismiss the unimaginable plight and misery of the slaves; yet to argue that his cruel treatment of the slaves outweighs everything else would be disregarding his unequivocal contributions in bringing democracy and freedom to America. Instead, what I can attempt to do is to point out the fact that Americans seem to be struggling with this question too. Americans are indeed aware of the “dark secrets” of George Washington, yet little to no voices call for the Washington Monument to be taken down. Perhaps, the very title of “Founding National Hero” elevates the person in question to the ranks of an untouchable superman, who is immune to historical revisions. Alternatively, people may have simply accepted George Washington’s misdeeds as an unfortunate result of customary practices during this period in history. In any case, as much as Americans are aware of the moral imperfections of their Founding Father, they are not as willing to point it out. Nonetheless, it seems that George Washington will continue to be remembered as the founding President and national hero of United States of America. Perhaps some new movement will emerge in the future that will challenge even George Washington’s legacy. However, at this current stage, monuments dedicated to George Washington have continued to withstand the test of time, and will likely continue to be part of American heritage and architecture for decades to come.
